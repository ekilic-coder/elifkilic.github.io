Heat Stress Across Latin America

Extreme heat is a silent threat whose impacts often go unnoticed until communities experience spikes in dehydration, heat stroke, kidney disease, and other illnesses. Central America and Mexico are particularly susceptible because many people live in poorly planned urban areas without access to clean water and sanitation, and high poverty levels limit adaptation options.

This page showcases preliminary analyses from our ongoing systematic review of heat stress research and interventions across Latin America. Below are country-specific analyses examining heat vulnerability, health impacts, and the populations most at risk. Each summary synthesizes available data on heat hazards and identifies critical knowledge gaps.

Country Analyses

Belize

The ThinkHazard database classifies Belize's extreme heat hazard as high, meaning prolonged exposure to dangerous temperatures is expected at least once in the next five years. Heat affects a variety of sectors—including health, energy, transport and agriculture—and disproportionately impacts urban populations and outdoor workers. In 2024 temperatures above 100 °F triggered forest fires, prompting the Belize Red Cross to provide hygiene kits and cash assistance.

Guatemala

Guatemala is one of the Central American countries with the highest risk of heat-related impacts. Heat waves and drought have contributed to a 622% increase in dengue cases between early 2023 and early 2024. The extreme heat hazard is classified as high, and vulnerability is amplified by rapid urbanisation, limited infrastructure and high poverty. Protecting children, older adults and outdoor workers is therefore a national priority.

Honduras

Like Guatemala, Honduras faces a high extreme heat hazard. The country recorded a 580% increase in dengue cases in early 2024 compared with the previous year due to a combination of heat and drought. High poverty, limited access to water and health services, and widespread outdoor work make the population particularly vulnerable.

El Salvador

El Salvador is listed alongside Guatemala as having the highest heat risk in Central America. Although detailed statistics are scarce, the country shares many of the same vulnerabilities: high urbanisation, inadequate infrastructure and limited health care capacity increase susceptibility to heat-related illness.

Nicaragua

Nicaragua does not have a publicly available hazard classification but experiences the same regional challenges: poor housing, lack of potable water and high poverty leave many communities exposed to extreme heat and humidity. Evidence on heat-related health impacts remains limited, yet studies suggest that heat waves can exacerbate dehydration, heat stroke and other illnesses.

Costa Rica

Costa Rica is generally considered less vulnerable due to stronger institutions, but rising temperatures and humidity still pose risks to outdoor workers and urban residents. As in the rest of the region, vulnerable groups include children, pregnant women and older adults. More research is needed to quantify how climate change will affect heat-related illnesses in the coming decades.

Panama

Limited hazard data exist for Panama, but the country is experiencing increasingly hot summers and humidity. With large urban centres on both coasts and a booming service economy, heat exposure will impact workers in construction, tourism and logistics. Ensuring access to cooling and hydration for vulnerable populations will be critical.

Mexico

Mexico has been hit by a multiyear drought since the winter of 2022–2023. By May 2024 nearly 76% of the country was experiencing drought, making it the most widespread drought since 2011. The drought has intensified heat waves, causing water scarcity and agricultural losses; central and southern Mexico—home to major population centres—have been particularly affected. Vulnerable populations include outdoor workers, farmers and those without air conditioning or access to reliable water sources.
